The problem

The problem isn't the training. It's everything around it.

Organizations spend enormous sums teaching people things that never make it into the work. By most estimates, close to half of all corporate training never changes what anyone actually does on the job.

The usual fix is to make the training better — sharper content, slicker delivery, a new platform. It rarely moves the needle, because the gap was never really about the content. People forget. They get pulled back into old routines. Their managers reward the old behavior. The systems around them don't budge. The learning happened; the doing didn't.

That gap has a name — the transfer problem — and almost everyone treats it as something to worry about after the training is built. That's the mistake.

The approach

What learning architecture is

Learning architecture is designing for behavior change from the start — not just the lesson, but the whole system the lesson has to live inside. It begins with a different question. Not "what should people know?" but "what do we need people to do differently, and what has to be true for that to actually happen?"

That means working across the things training usually ignores: how motivated people are to change, what their managers and teams reward, how the work itself is structured, what gets measured, and where the old habits are anchored. Get those right and the teaching has somewhere to land. Get them wrong and no amount of good content survives contact with Monday morning.

It's closer to strategy and systems design than to building a course. The goal was never a better course anyway. It's a measurable change in how an organization works.

Who this is for

Three kinds of people keep finding their way here.

Corporate L&D

Heads of learning and capability who are done reporting completion rates and need to show that programs change behavior — and the business numbers that follow from it. If you're under pressure to prove ROI on learning, the problem usually isn't your content. It's the architecture around it.

EdTech founders & investors

People building and backing learning products that have to prove outcomes, not just engagement. Real learning science and transfer design is often the line between a tool people open and a tool that demonstrably works — which is exactly what buyers, and your next round, will ask you to show.

Universities & education leaders

Provosts, deans, and program leaders trying to close the distance between what students learn and what employers need them to do. Curriculum built for transfer, and for genuine partnership with industry, is what turns a degree into capability someone can point to.
